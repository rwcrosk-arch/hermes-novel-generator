## 1. Vision & Agent Roles

A novel generated by multiple agentic LLMs with specialized roles. The system improves itself recursively: auditor agents critique output and feed improvements back into prompts, state schemas, and agent coordination over time.

### The Five Roles

| Role | Purpose | Creates? | Reviews? |
|------|---------|----------|----------|
| **Orchestrator** | High-level planning, task delegation, progress tracking, chapter sequencing, outline revision, cross-scene continuity review | Outlines, scene lists, task assignments, state updates | Whether the story is progressing toward its arc; chapter-level pacing and balance |
| **Storyteller/DM** | Central narrative control — world-building, scene briefs, writing narrative prose, adjudicating character conflicts, plot advancement | Scene briefs, scene structure guidance, narrative prose, world state updates | Whether scenes serve the story |
| **Lore Auditor** | Checks all narrative output for lore consistency, plot holes, character consistency, world rule violations, arc pacing, and thematic coherence | Structured audit reports (pass/fail per category) | All narrative output against accumulated world state |
| **Character Agents** | Each embodies a specific character with authentic voice, motivations, behaviors, and relationships. They review their own dialogue and actions in Storyteller drafts to ensure authenticity. | Voice-check feedback on Storyteller drafts | N/A (they ARE the characters) |
| **Prose Stylist** | Post-weaving polish pass — tightens prose, enforces genre tone, checks voice differentiation, removes clunky stitching artifacts | Polished scene prose | Prose quality, voice distinctness, genre consistency |

### Why This Role Set Works

The DM (Dungeon Master) metaphor is the right frame. The Storyteller sets up situations and weaves contributions, just like a DM narrates the world. Character agents don't decide plot direction — they respond authentically to situations AND to each other, creating emergent narrative. Auditors are the "playtesters" who catch when the world stops making sense.

The Orchestrator Auditor role has been removed. Its outline-review and chapter-review functions are now owned by the Orchestrator (self-checks) and the Lore Auditor (cross-chapter continuity and arc validation). Two separate auditors created coordination overhead with poorly differentiated value — the Lore Auditor was already checking continuity and character consistency; adding subjective "pacing assessments" and "thematic presence" ratings produced advisory noise that the Orchestrator could simply reject. Concrete, pass/fail checks from a single auditor are more actionable than ambiguous dual-auditor scores. (Lens 3: Over-Engineering)

### What Changed and Why

- **Orchestrator Auditor role removed.** The original plan had six roles. The Orchestrator Auditor produced subjective assessments ("pacing: too_fast|good|too_slow", "arc_adherence: on_track|drifting|off_track") that the Orchestrator could reject without consequence. It added a full delegate_task cycle per chapter with no enforcement mechanism. Its outline-review checklist (arc completeness, character balance, theme presence) has been merged into the Orchestrator's M0 self-check. Its chapter-review checklist (cross-scene continuity, pacing, tension curve) has been merged into the Lore Auditor's post-chapter review. This eliminates ~12 delegate calls per novel with no quality loss. (Lens 3: Over-Engineering)
- **Orchestrator now owns cross-scene continuity review.** Previously the Orchestrator Auditor did this; now the Orchestrator does it after chapter assembly, before the Lore Auditor's final pass. The Orchestrator has read access to everything and already controls chapter structure, so it's the natural owner. (Lens 2: Missing Pieces)
- **Lore Auditor scope expanded** to include arc adherence, dramatic tension curve, and thematic coherence checks across scenes within a chapter. These were previously the Orchestrator Auditor's domain. A single auditor with a comprehensive checklist is more reliable than two auditors with overlapping but incomplete checklists. (Lens 5: Quality Mechanisms)
- **Character Agents' purpose updated** from "respond in turn-based dialogue" to "review Storyteller drafts for voice authenticity." The turn-based model was computationally infeasible and produced stilted prose. The voice-check model is faster and preserves character authenticity without rigid turn structure. (Lens 1: Architectural Flaws)

### What Changed and Why

**Turn-based dialogue loop eliminated.** The original plan had a per-beat, round-robin character turn system where the Storyteller narrated a beat, then each character agent responded, then the Storyteller wove responses into prose. This produced 12-18 delegate_task calls per scene and created stilted, game-like prose where characters took neat turns rather than overlapping, interrupting, and behaving organically. The new model: Storyteller writes the full scene draft using character profiles (3-5 beats in one call), then character agents review only their own lines for voice violations (1 call per character, batched where possible). This reduces calls from ~18 to ~5 per scene — a 70% reduction — and produces more natural prose because the Storyteller controls flow, timing, and environmental beats directly. (Lens 1: Architectural Flaws, Lens 3: Over-Engineering)

**Scene brief validation failure path added.** The original flow chart showed "If brief fails: revise (max 1 revision)" but did not show what happened if validation failed twice. The new flow chart explicitly shows: "If fail: revise once. If still fail: flag scene, skip to next." This prevents the system from getting stuck on broken briefs. (Lens 4: Vague Specifications)

**Adjudication mechanism simplified.** The original plan had explicit conflict adjudication with `adjudication.md` notes when characters wanted contradictory outcomes. In practice, this only matters for high-stakes conflict scenes. The new model handles this implicitly: the Storyteller controls the narrative and resolves conflicts according to the scene brief's `intended_outcome`. For scenes where character goals genuinely collide in ways the brief doesn't resolve, the Storyteller escalates to the Orchestrator with a specific question. The formal adjudication protocol and file format have been removed as unnecessary overhead. (Lens 3: Over-Engineering)

**Chapter-level Lore Auditor pass added.** After the Orchestrator's cross-scene review, the Lore Auditor performs a final chapter-level check for cross-scene continuity issues that scene-level auditing missed. This was previously the Orchestrator Auditor's job; now it's part of the Lore Auditor's comprehensive review. (Lens 2: Missing Pieces, Lens 5: Quality Mechanisms)

**Character agent step redefined.** Step 4 is now a voice-check pass, not turn-based participation. Each character agent receives: their full profile + the scene draft + instructions to flag any of their own dialogue or action descriptions that feel out of character. They do NOT rewrite; they flag. The Storyteller revises flagged lines. This preserves authenticity without the overhead of turn-based generation. (Lens 1: Architectural Flaws)

### Revision Limit Detail

The "max 3 revision rounds" between Storyteller and Lore Auditor exists to prevent infinite loops. If after 3 rounds the auditor still flags issues:
- The scene is saved with an `ISSUES:` header listing unresolved problems
- The Orchestrator is notified and can decide whether to skip the scene, restructure it, or mark it for human review
- The generation loop continues — stalemate doesn't block progress

### Targeted Revision Protocol

When the Lore Auditor flags issues in a scene:

1. The auditor produces a structured report listing each issue by category, severity, and the SPECIFIC PASSAGE (quoted text) that's problematic
2. The Storyteller receives: the original scene draft + the audit report (NOT a vague "fix the problems" instruction)
3. The Storyteller revises ONLY the flagged passages. Untouched passages must remain verbatim identical
4. On re-audit, the Lore Auditor checks ONLY the revised passages and their immediate context (±2 sentences). Previously passing passages are assumed still valid unless a revision introduced an obvious cascading error (e.g., a character pronoun change that affects later references)
5. After re-audit passes, the Prose Stylist performs a **narrow re-pass** — re-examining ONLY the revised passages plus ±1 paragraph of surrounding context, not the entire scene. This prevents the Stylist from inadvertently altering already-polished prose during a revision cycle

If the Storyteller believes an auditor flag is wrong, the Storyteller notes the disagreement in the revision rather than ignoring the flag. This makes disagreements discoverable in the audit log.

### Cost and Latency Awareness

Each scene generates approximately `1 + characters + 1 + characters + 1 + 1 + 1` delegate_task calls (brief generation + character strategies + Storyteller weave + character voice-checks + audit + stylist + state update). For a 4-beat scene with 3 characters, this is ~10 calls.

With 3-4 scenes per chapter and 8-12 chapters, total delegate_task calls for a short novel range from ~250-400. Each call is a fresh sub-agent invocation with its own context window.

**Mitigations**:
- Character strategy generation can be batched into one delegate_task call per scene (all characters plan simultaneously, each in their own isolated context), reducing calls by ~30%
- Brief validation and state updates are lightweight calls (small context)
- The system is designed to allow resuming from any completed scene — a failed chapter doesn't require restarting the novel

## 2c. Scene Sandbox Model (Character Agency Preservation)

The v5 plan initially replaced turn-based dialogue with a passive voice-check model: the Storyteller wrote everything, and characters only reviewed their lines afterward. This eliminates the "separate simulated minds" quality that makes the output special. Stories become bland because the Storyteller is the sole author of every decision, every reaction, every surprise.

The **Scene Sandbox Model** is the middle ground. It preserves character agency without the computational infeasibility of per-beat turns:

### How It Works

1. **Storyteller writes the scene brief** with beat structure, just as before.
2. **Character agents generate scene strategies** (parallel, all at once). Each character agent receives:
   - Their full persona profile
   - The complete scene brief (setting, conflict, beats, their goal)
   - What they know and don't know
   - Their current emotional state and relationships
   
   Each character agent outputs a **scene strategy**: a beat-by-beat plan of what they intend to do, say, and react to. They do NOT see other characters' strategies. They plan in isolation, just like real people entering a room with their own intentions.

3. **Storyteller receives ALL strategies** and writes the full scene prose. The Storyteller is now a director with actors' intentions, not a puppeteer with puppets. The Storyteller:
   - Decides when characters speak over each other, interrupt, or act simultaneously
   - Resolves conflicts according to dramatic logic (who has more at stake, whose arc needs the win)
   - Adds environmental beats, sensory details, and pacing that no character agent would think to include
   - Can be SURPRISED by a character's strategy — the character might plan something the Storyteller didn't anticipate, creating genuinely emergent narrative

4. **Character agents do voice-check** on the woven draft, just as in the base v5 loop. This catches any lines that feel wrong after the weave.

### Why This Preserves the Special Sauce

**Characters still have independent minds.** In step 2, each character plans their scene strategy without seeing the others' plans. Marcus might plan to confront Elyria about the embryos. Elyria might plan to redirect the conversation to Veridia's habitability. Kira might plan to hide and observe. The Storyteller didn't decide any of these — the characters did. The Storyteller only decides how they collide.

**Characters can surprise the Storyteller.** A character agent might decide to do something unexpected: lie when the Storyteller expected honesty, attack when the Storyteller expected negotiation, break down crying when the Storyteller expected stoicism. This is the emergent quality that turn-based dialogue was supposed to produce — and the Scene Sandbox produces it with 60% fewer API calls.

**The Storyteller controls prose quality.** The biggest failure of the turn-based model was that the Storyteller couldn't control pacing, interruptions, environmental beats, or simultaneous action. In the Scene Sandbox, the Storyteller writes the actual prose, so these elements are handled naturally. Characters supply the "what"; the Storyteller supplies the "how."

### Computational Savings vs. Turn-Based Model

| Approach | Calls per 3-char scene | Agency Level | Prose Quality |
|----------|----------------------|-------------|---------------|
| Turn-based (v4) | ~18 | High | Low (stilted) |
| Voice-check only (v5 base) | ~5 | Low (none) | High |
| **Scene Sandbox (v5.5)** | **~10** | **High** | **High** |

The Scene Sandbox is the sweet spot: characters are active participants (not reviewers), but the Storyteller writes the prose (not a weaver stitching turns together).

### When to Use Voice-Check Only

For scenes with only one character (internal monologue, solo action), skip the strategy generation. The Storyteller writes the draft directly. This saves ~2-3 calls per scene in single-character chapters.

### What Changed and Why

**Added the Scene Sandbox Model as §2c.** The v5 base plan used voice-check only, which killed character agency. The Scene Sandbox restores it by making characters active strategy generators before the Storyteller writes prose. This is the most important change in the v5.5 revision — without it, the system produces competent but soulless prose. (Lens 1: Architectural Flaws, Lens 5: Quality Mechanisms)

**Updated the generation loop** to show Scene Sandbox as the default character interaction model, with voice-check-only as the fallback for single-character scenes.

## 2b. Beat Structure Flexibility

Scenes need different numbers of beats depending on their narrative role. The schema uses a flexible beat list, not a fixed 4-beat template:

- **Climactic scenes**: 6-8 beats (building tension across multiple escalation points)
- **Standard scenes**: 3-5 beats (hook, development, turning point, resolution)
- **Transitional scenes**: 1-2 beats (brief connective tissue between major scenes)
- **Reaction scenes**: 2-3 beats (character processing events, emotional beats)

The beat structure in the scene brief specifies the number and type of beats. The Storyteller chooses the beat count based on the scene's role in the chapter. The Orchestrator's scene list should annotate each scene with its narrative role (climactic, standard, transitional, reaction) so the Storyteller knows what scale of beat structure to produce.

```yaml
        passage: "quoted text from scene draft that's problematic"
        suggested_fix: "how to resolve it"
        resolved: true/false
        resolution: "how it was fixed or why it was deferred"
```

### What Changed and Why

**`state_version` field added to `meta`.** The original plan had no mechanism to detect accidental state overwrites or corruption. If two agents write to `novel_state.yaml` concurrently (or if a human edits it while generation is running), the last write wins silently. The `state_version` increments on every update. The update protocol checks the version before writing; if the on-disk version is higher than expected, the update aborts and flags for human review. This prevents race conditions and accidental overwrites. (Lens 1: Architectural Flaws)

**State update protocol made concrete (see below).** The original plan said "Orchestrator updates state" with a vague "YAML patch" instruction. The new protocol specifies append-only rules for `world.timeline` and `audit_log`, explicit patch format for character updates, and a validation function. Without concrete rules, different Orchestrator invocations would use inconsistent formats, making the state unreadable and causing parsing failures. (Lens 4: Vague Specifications)

**Lore Auditor `type` expanded** to include `pacing` and `theme` (previously only the Orchestrator Auditor checked these). Since the Orchestrator Auditor role was removed, these checks must live somewhere. The Lore Auditor is the natural home — it already reads every scene and has full world context. (Lens 2: Missing Pieces)

**Scene status enum updated** to reflect the new generation loop: `drafting` replaces `character_turns` and `weaving` (the Storyteller now drafts in one step); `voice_check` is the new phase where character agents review their lines. (Lens 1: Architectural Flaws)

### Why YAML, Not JSON

YAML is human-readable, supports comments (critical for WIP notes), and is easy for both humans and agents to edit. JSON is for APIs; YAML is for collaboration. The state file will be read and modified constantly — it must be legible at a glance.

### State Access Rules

- **Orchestrator**: writes to `outline`, `chapter_plan`, state updates after scenes, reads everything (with summaries for older chapters)
- **Storyteller**: writes to `scene.brief`, `scene.draft` (file), `chapter_transition_prose`, reads `outline`, `characters`, `world`, `summaries`, reads/writes `world.locations`, `world.timeline`
- **Character Agents**: read their own character entry + scene brief + scene draft, write only their voice-check feedback
- **Lore Auditor**: read-only access to everything (with summaries for older chapters), writes only to `audit_log`
- **Prose Stylist**: reads `scene.draft` (file) + genre/tone/voice info from `characters` + `meta.genre`, writes to `scene.final_prose` (file) and `scene.stylist_notes`

### State Update Protocol

The state update after each scene (loop step 9) is performed by the **Orchestrator**. The update follows this exact process:

1. **Orchestrator reads**: the completed scene final, the audit log for this scene, and current `novel_state.yaml`
2. **Orchestrator increments** `meta.state_version` by 1
3. **Orchestrator applies updates** using these rules:
   - **`world.timeline`**: APPEND only. Add new events as new list items. Never modify or delete existing timeline entries.
   - **`audit_log`**: APPEND only. Add the current scene's audit as a new entry.
   - **`characters[].relationships[]`**: UPDATE in place. For each changed relationship, specify: `character_id`, `with`, `field_to_change`, `old_value`, `new_value`. The validation step checks that `old_value` matches the current state.
   - **`characters[].growth.chapters[]`**: APPEND only. Add a new chapter entry with `state_after` and `key_realization`.
   - **`summaries.chapter_summaries[]`**: UPDATE in place if entry exists (current chapter), else APPEND.
   - **`summaries.character_arcs_so_far`**: UPDATE in place. Overwrite the one-liner for each character who appeared.
   - **`chapters[].scenes[].status`**: UPDATE in place. Set to `"complete"` or `"flagged"`.
4. **Orchestrator validates** the update:
   - Check that `meta.state_version` on disk matches the expected version (pre-increment)
   - Check that no `old_value` in character updates contradicts the current state
   - Check that all required fields (`last_modified`, `current_chapter`, `current_scene`) are present
   - Check that `world.timeline` has no duplicate events (same chapter, scene, and event text)
   - Check that `characters[].id` values are unique
5. **If validation passes**: Orchestrator writes the updated `novel_state.yaml`
6. **If validation fails**: Orchestrator logs the specific validation failure, restores from the last snapshot, and flags the scene for human review. The generation loop pauses until the state issue is resolved.

This is NOT optional — it is a required step that happens after every single scene. Without it, the system has no memory and characters will contradict earlier chapters.

### Context Window Budget

Each agent invocation should receive no more than ~8K tokens of context. The budget is:

| Context Component | Token Budget | Source |
|---|---|---|
| System prompt / role | ~500 | Agent template |
| Scene brief (current scene) | ~500 | Generated fresh |
| Character profiles (active characters) | ~1000 | From state, truncated to essentials |
| Recent chapter summary | ~500 | From `summaries` |
| World rules | ~500 | From state |
| Relevant relationship states | ~300 | From state |
| Writing guidelines | ~200 | Static |
| Scene draft (for voice-check or audit) | ~2000 | From file |
| **Total** | **~5500** | Leaves headroom for output |

For chapters older than 2 back, ONLY summaries are included — no full prose, no detailed timeline entries.

#### Context Overflow Guardrail

Before spawning any agent, the orchestrator MUST estimate the context size. If the estimated context exceeds 6K tokens (leaving 2K for the agent's response within the ~8K budget):

1. First, apply the Profile Truncation rules (see below)
2. If still over budget, drop character voice examples (keep voice_description only)
3. If still over budget, drop world rules and use only recent chapter summary
4. If still over budget, flag the scene for human review — the novel may have grown too complex for the current approach

This guardrail prevents silent context overflow, which would produce garbage output without any error signal.

#### Character Profile Truncation

When building context for a character agent or the Storyteller, truncate character profiles to essentials:

- **Always include**: `id`, `name`, `role`, `voice_description`, all `behavioral_constraints`, `knowledge_boundaries`, `misbeliefs`, current `relationships` status (not full history), current `growth.current_state`
- **Include if present in this scene**: full `voice_examples`, scene-specific `knowledge.learned_facts` relevant to current plot
- **Truncate or omit**: `growth.arc` (summarize to one line), `growth.chapters` history (only include current and previous chapter), full `relationship.key_moments` (only recent)

This keeps character profiles under ~500 tokens each while preserving the information that matters for in-scene behavior.

## 4. Character Agent Design

A character agent is more than a system prompt. It's a complete persona specification that makes the character behave authentically and distinctly.

### Character Agent Prompt Template (Scene Strategy Mode)

This is the prompt used when a character agent generates their scene strategy (step 3 of the loop). Each character receives the same scene brief but plans independently — they do NOT see other characters' strategies.

```markdown
# You ARE {character_name}

You are not narrating — you ARE this person. Your job is to plan what you will do in the upcoming scene.

## Voice
{voice_description}

### Speech Examples
- When greeting a stranger: "{example_dialogue_1}"
- When under stress: "{example_dialogue_2}"  
- When reflecting quietly: "{example_dialogue_3}"
- When angry: "{example_dialogue_4}"
- When being dishonest: "{example_dialogue_5}"

## Identity
- Role: {protagonist/antagonist/supporting}
- Core motivation: {primary_drive}
- Deepest fear: {deepest_fear}
- Secret: {what_you_hide}

## Behavioral absolutes
You would NEVER: {constraint_1}, {constraint_2}
You always: {habit_1}, {habit_2}

## Current state
- Emotional state: {current_emotional_state}
- What you just experienced: {previous_scene_summary}
- What you know right now: {list_of_facts_character_has_learned}
- What you DON'T know: {knowledge_boundaries}
- Misbeliefs you hold: {things_you_believe_wrongly}

## Relationships right now
{relationship_summary_with_each_other_character_present}

## Scene context
- Setting: {where_and_when}
- Total beats in this scene: {N}
- Situation: {what's_happening_at_scene_start}
- What you want from this scene: {character_scene_goal}
- Other characters present: {list_of_other_characters}
- What you know about them: {what_this_character_knows_about_others}

## Your Task

Plan your actions for each beat of this scene. You are planning IN CHARACTER — you don't know what the other characters will do. You only know your own intentions, fears, and goals.

For EACH beat, specify:
1. **Action**: What you physically do (or don't do)
2. **Dialogue** (if any): The exact words you would say, in your voice
3. **Internal reaction**: What you feel/think but don't show
4. **Intention**: Why you're doing this — what you want to achieve

Output format:
```
BEAT 1 ({beat_type}: {beat_description}):
- Action: <what you do>
- Dialogue: "<what you say>"
- Internal: <what you think/feel>
- Intention: <why you're doing this>

BEAT 2 ...
```

Rules:
1. Stay in character. Always. No narrative asides.
2. Do not reveal information your character doesn't know.
3. React authentically — if something would upset your character, show it.
4. Plan based on what YOU want, not what would make a good scene.
5. You may plan to lie, hide, attack, flee, break down, or do anything your character would actually do.
6. Keep dialogue concise — natural speech, not monologues.
7. If the scene brief says another character does something specific, react to THAT, not to what you wish they would do.
```

This is the prompt used when a character agent reviews their lines in the woven draft (step 5 of the loop).

```markdown
You are not narrating — you ARE this person. Your job is to review a scene draft and flag any dialogue or action attributed to you that feels OUT OF CHARACTER.

## Voice
{voice_description}

- When greeting a stranger: "{example_dialogue_1}"
- When under stress: "{example_dialogue_2}"  
- When reflecting quietly: "{example_dialogue_3}"
- When angry: "{example_dialogue_4}"
- When being dishonest: "{example_dialogue_5}"

## Identity
- Role: {protagonist/antagonist/supporting}
- Core motivation: {primary_drive}
- Deepest fear: {deepest_fear}
- Secret: {what_you_hide}

## Behavioral absolutes
You would NEVER: {constraint_1}, {constraint_2}
You always: {habit_1}, {habit_2}

## Current state
- Emotional state: {current_emotional_state}
- What you just experienced: {previous_scene_summary}
- What you know right now: {list_of_facts_character_has_learned}
- What you DON'T know: {knowledge_boundaries}
- Misbeliefs you hold: {things_you_believe_wrongly}

## Relationships right now
{relationship_summary_with_each_other_character_present}

## Scene context
- Setting: {where_and_when}
- Beat: {which_beat_of_the_scene_we_are_in} of {total_beats_in_this_scene}
- Situation: {what's_happening_right_now}
- What you want from this scene: {character_scene_goal}

## The Scene Draft
{full_scene_draft}

## Your Task
Read the scene draft. Identify every piece of dialogue spoken by {character_name} and every action or internal thought attributed to {character_name}.

For EACH of your lines, ask:
1. Would I say it this way? (Check against voice_description and speech examples)
2. Would I do this? (Check against behavioral absolutes)
3. Do I know this? (Check against knowledge_boundaries — am I acting on information I shouldn't have?)
4. Does this match my current emotional state and scene goal?

Output format:
```
PASS: <count of lines that feel correct>
FLAGS:
- [Line N]: "<quoted line>" — Problem: <voice violation / behavioral break / knowledge leak / emotional mismatch>
  Suggested revision: "<how this line should read>"
```

If ALL your lines feel correct, output only: ALL_PASS
```

### What Changed and Why

**Prompt purpose changed from "respond in turn" to "review for authenticity."** The original template had characters generate their own dialogue and actions in response to beat-by-beat narration. This required 3-5 turns per beat and produced rigid, game-like prose. The new template gives characters the full scene draft (written by the Storyteller) and asks them to flag violations. This preserves character voice without the overhead of turn-based generation. (Lens 1: Architectural Flaws, Lens 3: Over-Engineering)

**"Other characters' recent actions" section removed.** This section was critical for turn-based interaction but is unnecessary for voice-check mode. The character agent reads the full draft and sees all other characters' actions directly. Removing this simplifies the prompt and reduces context size. (Lens 3: Over-Engineering)

**"Beat" context retained.** Characters still need to know where in the scene structure they are, because their voice and behavior may shift from opening hook to resolution. The total beat count is included so characters know how far through the scene they are. (Lens 4: Vague Specifications)

**Output format made explicit.** The original template said "Respond only as {character_name} would respond" but gave no format guidance. The new template requires either `ALL_PASS` or structured `FLAGS` with quoted lines and suggested revisions. This makes the feedback actionable for the Storyteller. (Lens 4: Vague Specifications)

### Voice Differentiation Test

After generating a scene where multiple characters speak, run this check:
> "Strip all dialogue attributions (character names, action beats that identify the speaker). Can you tell which character said which line based on voice alone?"

If you can't, the voices aren't distinct enough. Revise persona descriptions.

Additionally, run a **conflict authenticity test**:
> "In scenes where two characters disagree, does each character's argument reflect THEIR specific motivations and fears, or could the arguments be swapped between characters without changing the scene?"

If arguments are swappable, character motivations aren't specific enough.

## 5. Prose Stylist Specification

The Prose Stylist is a late-pass role that takes a scene draft (after Lore Auditor approval) and polishes it. This role exists because the Storyteller's job is narrative architecture, not prose beauty.

### When It Runs

- After each scene passes Lore Audit (full scene pass)
- After targeted revision cycles (narrow re-pass on revised passages only — see below)

### Input

- The scene draft (from Storyteller, or the revised portion + ±1 paragraph context after a revision cycle)
- Character voice profiles (from state)
- Genre and tone guidelines (from meta)

### What It Checks

1. **Voice differentiation**: Strip dialogue attributions. Can you tell who's talking?
2. **Prose rhythm**: Sentence length variation, paragraph rhythm, avoiding repetitive structures.
3. **Stitching artifacts**: Remove obvious seams where character responses were joined. "He said... She said... He then said..." patterns.
4. **Show don't tell**: Flag places where emotions are stated instead of demonstrated.
5. **Genre tone**: Does the prose match the declared genre? (Thriller prose differs from romance prose.)
6. **Opening hooks**: Does each scene open with something that grabs attention?
7. **Scene transitions**: Do scene endings pull the reader forward?

### Output

- `scene_final.md` — the polished prose
- Stylist notes in state — what was changed and why (for the Storyteller to learn from)

### Narrow Re-Pass After Targeted Revision

When the Prose Stylist runs after a targeted revision cycle (not a first-pass polish), it receives:

1. The revised passages (as identified by the Lore Auditor)
2. ±1 paragraph of context surrounding each revised passage
3. The Stylist's own notes from the first-pass polish (so it knows what it already changed and why)

The Stylist re-examines ONLY this narrowed scope. It does NOT re-polish the entire scene. This prevents cascading corruption — if the Stylist re-polishes already-approved prose, it risks altering passages that were intentionally crafted during the first pass.

If the Stylist identifies a problem in an untouched passage during the narrow re-pass, it flags it in `stylist_notes` with type `narrative_issue` but does NOT modify it. The next full chapter review can address it.

### Content Boundary Rule

The Prose Stylist may ONLY modify prose quality. It must NOT change narrative content, plot events, or character decisions. If the Stylist identifies a narrative content problem, it flags it in `stylist_notes` with type `narrative_issue` but does not modify it. Narrative content changes go back to the Storyteller as a revision request.

This is a hard rule, not a guideline. The boundary prevents the Stylist from accidentally rewriting plot or changing character intentions during what should be a prose pass.

## 6. Implementation Milestones

Progress by what works, not by calendar.

### M0: Orchestrator produces outline from seed
- User provides a novel seed (premise, genre, themes)
- Orchestrator agent generates a complete outline with chapters, key events, character list
- Orchestrator self-checks outline for arc completeness, character balance, plausibility
- Output saved to `novel_state.yaml`
- **Done when**: Given a seed, the system produces a coherent outline that a human would want to read

### M1: Storyteller produces scene briefs from outline
- Given an outline and chapter plan, Storyteller generates scene briefs WITH beat structure and character goals
- Each brief covers: setting, conflict, characters present, intended outcome, beat structure, character goals
- Storyteller self-validates each brief against chapter plan, world state, and character knowledge boundaries
- **Done when**: Scene briefs are specific enough that a Storyteller could write a full scene from them without asking clarifying questions

### M2: Storyteller generates scene drafts
- Storyteller writes full scene prose from briefs and character profiles
- Maintains tone, handles action beats, manages pacing within the scene
- Characters enter and exit at specified beats
- **Done when**: Prose reads like a novel, not like a summary. A blind test reader cannot tell it was generated by an AI.

### M3: Character voice-check pass works
- Character agents review their own lines in Storyteller drafts
- Flag voice violations, behavioral breaks, knowledge leaks
- Storyteller revises flagged lines
- **Done when**: Two characters in the same scene produce recognizably different dialogue. Voice differentiation test passes.

### M4: Lore Auditor catches violations
- Given accumulated world state (with summaries for older chapters) and a new scene, Lore Auditor identifies:
  - Continuity errors (character locations, timeline, event references)
  - Character voice breaks (dialogue that doesn't match the persona)
  - World rule violations (magic system breaks, technology anachronisms)
  - Knowledge leaks (character acting on information they shouldn't have)
  - Pacing issues (scene too fast/slow for its narrative role)
  - Theme absence (declared themes not present)
- Auditor flags SPECIFIC PASSAGES with suggested fixes
- **Done when**: Auditor correctly flags at least 3 of 5 intentionally planted errors in a test scene

### M5: Targeted revision works
- Storyteller revises ONLY flagged passages (not the whole scene)
- Re-audit checks only revised passages and their immediate context
- Disagreements between Storyteller and Auditor are logged, not silently ignored
- Prose Stylist performs narrow re-pass on revised passages only (not the full scene)
- **Done when**: Revision cycle converges in ≤2 rounds for 80% of test cases, and doesn't introduce new errors in untouched passages

### M6: State update loop works end-to-end
- After each scene, Orchestrator updates novel_state.yaml with new facts, relationship changes, character growth, timeline entries
- State update is validated (no contradictions, no missing required fields, version check passes)
- Characters in later scenes act on information they learned in earlier scenes
- **Done when**: A character in scene 3 references a fact established in scene 1 without being told to do so, and a character in scene 2 does NOT reference a fact they haven't learned yet

### M7: Full loop generates one complete chapter
- Orchestrator -> scene list -> scene briefs -> brief validation -> Storyteller draft -> character voice-check -> Lore Audit -> targeted revision -> styling -> state update
- No human intervention during the loop
- **Done when**: A reader can read the generated chapter without obvious signs of AI stitching

### M8: Multi-chapter generation with state persistence
- The full loop runs across multiple chapters
- State accumulates correctly (characters learn things, relationships evolve, timeline advances)
- Context management keeps agent inputs under budget
- **Done when**: A 5-chapter novella maintains consistent world state throughout

### M9+: Quality improvements
- Richer character personas with more voice examples
- Edge case handling in the generation loop
- Console progress display
- State snapshot and recovery (see Failure Modes)
- Eventually: web visualization, relationship graphs, timeline viewers

### What Changed and Why

**M2 simplified.** The original M2 was "Storyteller validates scene briefs" and M3/M4 were "Character agents respond to scene briefs in-character" and "Turn-based dialogue within scenes." These three milestones have been replaced by M2 (Storyteller generates drafts) and M3 (character voice-check). The brief validation is now part of M1 (a self-check step, not a separate milestone). The turn-based model was computationally infeasible and never worked in practice; the voice-check model is validated by M3. This reduces 12 milestones to 9 without losing any proof points. (Lens 3: Over-Engineering)

**M6 expanded** to include state version validation and contradiction checking. The original M6 only checked that characters remembered facts; it didn't check for state corruption, race conditions, or validation failures. The new M6 includes the full validation protocol from §3. Without this, the multi-chapter goal at M8 is built on sand — a corrupted state in chapter 2 breaks everything downstream. (Lens 1: Architectural Flaws)

**M5 criterion clarified.** "Doesn't introduce new errors in untouched passages" was previously implicit. Now it's explicit. The targeted revision protocol is the core quality mechanism; without validation that it doesn't break untouched prose, the system would trade one bug for another. (Lens 5: Quality Mechanisms)

**M9+ trimmed.** Removed "Orchestrator Auditor reviews pacing and arc across chapters" since the Orchestrator Auditor role was eliminated. The Orchestrator now owns cross-chapter continuity as part of its normal workflow. (Lens 3: Over-Engineering)

## 7. Failure Modes & Recovery

| Failure | Detection | Recovery |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Agent produces garbage output | Output fails basic validation (empty, off-topic, format broken) | Retry with same agent (max 2 retries). If still garbage, flag scene and continue. |
| Lore Auditor and Storyteller disagree after 3 revision rounds | revision_count reaches 3 | Save scene with `ISSUES:` header. Orchestrator decides: skip, restructure, or flag for human review. |
| Character agent flags systemic issues (many lines wrong) | Voice-check finds >50% of a character's lines flagged | Indicates persona description is insufficient. Pause generation, revise character profile with more voice_examples and behavioral_constraints, then regenerate the scene. |
| Shared state file gets corrupted | YAML parse error, schema validation fails, or state_version mismatch | Restore from last known-good snapshot. All state writes create a backup of previous state before overwriting. |
| State update validation fails | Contradiction detected, missing required field, or state_version mismatch | Abort update. Restore from snapshot. Flag scene for human review. Generation pauses until resolved. |
| API call fails mid-generation | HTTP error, timeout | Retry with exponential backoff (3 attempts). If persistent, pause generation and log error for human review. |
| Agent context window overflow | Estimated context exceeds budget before agent spawn | Apply Context Overflow Guardrail (§3): truncate profiles, then drop voice examples, then drop world rules. If still over budget, flag scene for human review. |
| Infinite generation loop (agents keep revising forever) | Total revision rounds across all agents exceeds threshold | Hard cap: any scene gets max 3 revision cycles. Then it's done, flagged or not. |
| Characters produce unresolvable conflict | Two characters both have valid dramatic claims | Storyteller resolves according to scene brief's intended_outcome. If the brief doesn't specify, escalate to Orchestrator with a specific question. |
| Prose Stylist changes narrative content | Stylist's output adds/removes plot events or changes character intentions | Discard Stylist's version. Use the pre-Stylist draft. Add the narrative content flag to `stylist_notes` for Storyteller to address in next revision. |
| Scene brief is fundamentally broken | Brief validation fails twice (initial + one revision) | Escalate to Orchestrator to restructure the scene or merge/split with adjacent scenes. Don't send broken briefs to the Storyteller. |
| Orchestrator flags a chapter | Scene or chapter set to `flagged` status | Lore Auditor runs immediate review focusing on the specific issue that triggered the flag. Orchestrator decides next action. |

### What Changed and Why

**"Character agent flags systemic issues" failure mode added.** If a voice-check flags more than half of a character's lines, the problem is not the Storyteller — it's the character profile. The profile lacks sufficient voice_examples or behavioral_constraints to guide the Storyteller. Without this failure mode, the system would burn endless revision cycles trying to fix a fundamentally underspecified character. The recovery is to pause and improve the profile. (Lens 2: Missing Pieces, Lens 5: Quality Mechanisms)

**"State update validation fails" failure mode added.** The original plan had no specific recovery for when the state update itself fails validation. The update protocol (§3) now includes validation steps; if they fail, the system must restore from snapshot and pause. Without this, a bad state update would cascade silently into all future chapters. (Lens 1: Architectural Flaws)

**Orchestrator Auditor failure modes removed.** The original table had entries for Orchestrator Auditor disagreement and audit trigger failures. Since the Orchestrator Auditor role was eliminated, these failure modes are no longer relevant. (Lens 3: Over-Engineering)

### State Snapshotting Strategy

Every time a chapter completes successfully, snapshot the entire `novel_state.yaml`:

```
novel_state.yaml          <- current (latest chapter)
.snapshots/
  chapter_001.yaml        <- snapshot after chapter 1
  chapter_002.yaml        <- snapshot after chapter 2
```

Additionally, snapshot full scene prose separately:

```
scenes/
  ch01_s01_final.md
  ch01_s02_final.md
  ch02_s01_final.md
  ...
```

If state corruption is detected, restore from the latest snapshot and regenerate from that point.

## 9. Technical Implementation with Hermes

### Primary Approach: delegate_task + File-Based State

This is the ONLY approach for now. No Redis, no RabbitMQ, no WebSocket, no custom MCP server. Files on disk with `delegate_task` for agent spawning. YAGNI until we prove the core loop works.

### Main Orchestration Pipeline

```python
def generate_novel(seed, target="short_novel"):
    """Main entry point. Generates a complete novel from seed."""
    
    # M0: Generate outline
    state = generate_outline(seed)
    save_state(state)
    
    # M0: Orchestrator self-checks outline
    issues = orchestrator_self_check_outline(state)
    if issues:
        state = revise_outline(state, issues)
    
    # M1-M8: Generate each chapter
    num_chapters = len(state['outline']['chapter_plan'])
    for chapter_num in range(1, num_chapters + 1):
        state = load_state()  # Refresh from disk
        state = generate_chapter(state, chapter_num)
        save_state(state)
        snapshot_if_needed(chapter_num)
    
    return state


def generate_chapter(state, chapter_num):
    """Generate a complete chapter: briefs, draft, voice-check, audit, state update."""
    
    chapter_plan = state['outline']['chapter_plan'][chapter_num - 1]
    scene_list = chapter_plan['scene_list']
    
    for scene_num, scene_spec in enumerate(scene_list, start=1):
        # Step 1: Generate scene brief
        scene_brief = generate_scene_brief(state, chapter_num, scene_num, scene_spec)
        
        # Step 2: Validate scene brief
        validation = validate_scene_brief(scene_brief, state, chapter_num)
        if validation['status'] == 'failed':
            # One revision attempt
            scene_brief = revise_scene_brief(scene_brief, validation['issues'])
            validation = validate_scene_brief(scene_brief, state, chapter_num)
            if validation['status'] == 'failed':
                # Flag and skip
                state['chapters'][chapter_num-1]['scenes'][scene_num-1]['status'] = 'flagged'
                state['chapters'][chapter_num-1]['scenes'][scene_num-1]['issues_header'] = \
                    f"Brief validation failed: {validation['issues']}"
                continue
        
        # Step 3: Storyteller generates full scene draft
        scene_draft = generate_scene_draft(state, chapter_num, scene_num, scene_brief)
        save_scene_draft(chapter_num, scene_num, scene_draft)
        
        # Step 4: Character voice-checks
        character_feedback = collect_character_feedback(
            state, chapter_num, scene_num, scene_brief, scene_draft
        )
        
        # Step 5: Storyteller revises based on character feedback
        if character_feedback:
            scene_draft = revise_from_feedback(
                scene_draft, character_feedback, state
            )
            save_scene_draft(chapter_num, scene_num, scene_draft, version=2)
        
        # Step 6: Lore Auditor
        audit_result = lore_audit(state, chapter_num, scene_num, scene_draft)
        
        # Step 7: Targeted revision cycle (max 3 rounds)
        revision_round = 0
        while audit_result['status'] != 'pass' and revision_round < 3:
            scene_draft = targeted_revision(
                scene_draft, audit_result, state
            )
            # Narrow re-audit on revised passages only
            audit_result = lore_audit_revised(
                state, chapter_num, scene_num, scene_draft, 
                audit_result['revised_passages']
            )
            revision_round += 1
        
        if audit_result['status'] != 'pass':
            # Save with issues header
            scene_draft = f"ISSUES: {audit_result['unresolved']}\n\n{scene_draft}"
        
        # Step 8: Prose Stylist
        if revision_round == 0:
            # First pass — style full scene
            scene_final = prose_style(state, scene_draft, chapter_num, scene_num)
        else:
            # After revision — narrow re-pass on revised passages only
            revised_passages = audit_result.get('revised_passages', [])
            scene_final = prose_style_narrow(
                state, scene_draft, chapter_num, scene_num,
                revised_passages, 
                first_pass_notes=get_first_pass_stylist_notes(chapter_num, scene_num)
            )
        
        save_scene_final(chapter_num, scene_num, scene_final)
        
        # Step 9: State update (mandatory!)
        state = update_state_after_scene(
            state, chapter_num, scene_num, scene_final, audit_result
        )
        save_state(state)
    
    # Chapter assembly
    chapter_prose = assemble_chapter(state, chapter_num)
    save_chapter(chapter_num, chapter_prose)
    
    # Orchestrator cross-scene review
    review_notes = orchestrator_review_chapter(state, chapter_num, chapter_prose)
    
    # Lore Auditor final chapter review
    chapter_audit = lore_auditor_review_chapter(state, chapter_num)
    
    return state
```

### Context Overflow Guardrail

```python
def estimate_context_tokens(context_dict):
    """Estimate token count before spawning any agent.
    Prevents silent context overflow.
    """
    # Rough heuristic: 1 token ≈ 4 characters for English
    total_chars = 0
    for key, value in context_dict.items():
        if isinstance(value, str):
            total_chars += len(value)
        elif isinstance(value, (dict, list)):
            total_chars += len(json.dumps(value))
        else:
            total_chars += len(str(value))
    return total_chars // 4  # Token estimate


def enforce_context_budget(context_dict, budget_tokens=6000):
    """Ensure context stays within budget before spawning an agent.
    Returns (trimmed_context, was_truncated).
    Applies escalation steps from §3 if over budget.
    """
    estimated = estimate_context_tokens(context_dict)
    if estimated <= budget_tokens:
        return context_dict, False
    
    # Step 1: Apply profile truncation (already handled by truncate_character_profile)
    # This should already be done before calling this function.
    
    # Step 2: Drop character voice examples (keep voice_description only)
    if 'characters' in context_dict:
        for char in context_dict['characters']:
            if isinstance(char, dict) and 'voice_examples' in char:
                del char['voice_examples']
    
    estimated = estimate_context_tokens(context_dict)
    if estimated <= budget_tokens:
        return context_dict, True
    
    # Step 3: Drop world rules, keep only recent chapter summary
    if 'world_rules' in context_dict:
        del context_dict['world_rules']
    
    estimated = estimate_context_tokens(context_dict)
    if estimated <= budget_tokens:
        return context_dict, True
    
    # Step 4: Flag for human review — novel may be too complex
    context_dict['_context_overflow_flag'] = True
    return context_dict, True
```

```python
def generate_scene_strategies(state, chapter_num, scene_num, scene_brief):
    """Each character agent generates their scene strategy independently.
    
    Characters do NOT see each other's strategies. They plan in isolation,
    just like real people entering a room with their own intentions.
    """
    
    characters_present = scene_brief['characters_present']
    strategies = {}
    
    for char_id in characters_present:
        char_profile = truncate_character_profile(state, char_id)
        
        # Build other characters info (what THIS character knows about them)
        other_chars_info = []
        for other_id in characters_present:
            if other_id == char_id:
                continue
            other = get_character(state, other_id)
            rel = get_relationship(state, char_id, other_id)
            other_chars_info.append({
                'id': other_id,
                'name': other['name'],
                'relationship_type': rel['type'] if rel else 'unknown',
                'relationship_status': rel['status'] if rel else 'neutral',
                'what_i_know': get_character_knowledge(state, char_id, other_id)
            })
        
        context = {
            'character_profile': char_profile,
            'scene_brief': scene_brief,
            'other_characters': other_chars_info,
            'previous_scene_summary': get_previous_scene_summary(state, chapter_num, scene_num),
            'instructions': "Plan your actions for each beat. You don't know what others will do."
        }
        context, _ = enforce_context_budget(context)
        
        strategy = delegate_task(
            goal=f"Generate scene strategy as {char_profile['name']}",
            context=context,
            toolsets=['terminal', 'file']
        )
        
        strategies[char_id] = strategy
    
    return strategies


def generate_scene_draft(state, chapter_num, scene_num, scene_brief, character_strategies):
    """Storyteller writes full scene prose from brief + all character strategies."""

        "Characters supplied the 'what' — you supply the 'how.'"

    context, _ = enforce_context_budget(context)
    
    scene_draft = delegate_task(
        goal="Write a complete scene in narrative prose from character strategies",
        context=context,
        toolsets=['terminal', 'file']
    )
    
    return scene_draft


def collect_character_feedback(state, chapter_num, scene_num, scene_brief, scene_draft):
    """Each character agent reviews their own lines in the draft for authenticity."""
    
    characters_present = scene_brief['characters_present']
    feedback = []
    
    for char_id in characters_present:
        char_profile = truncate_character_profile(state, char_id)
        
        char_context = {
            'character_profile': char_profile,
            'scene_brief': scene_brief,
            'scene_draft': scene_draft,
            'instructions': "Review your lines. Flag voice violations, behavioral breaks, or knowledge leaks."
        }
        char_context, _ = enforce_context_budget(char_context)
        
        char_result = delegate_task(
            goal=f"Review lines as {char_profile['name']} for authenticity",
            context=char_context,
            toolsets=['terminal', 'file']
        )
        
        # Only collect if character found issues
        if 'ALL_PASS' not in char_result:
            feedback.append({
                'character': char_id,
                'feedback': char_result
            })
    
    return feedback


def revise_from_feedback(scene_draft, character_feedback, state):
```

```python
def update_state_after_scene(state, chapter_num, scene_num, scene_final, audit_result):
    """Orchestrator updates novel_state.yaml after a scene completes.
    
    This is NOT optional. It runs after every scene.
    Uses append-only rules and explicit patch format.
    """
    
    # Check version before updating
    current_version = state['meta']['state_version']
    disk_state = load_state()
    if disk_state['meta']['state_version'] != current_version:
        raise StateVersionMismatch(
            f"Expected version {current_version}, found {disk_state['meta']['state_version']}. "
            "Another process modified state. Aborting update."
        )
    
    # Increment version
    state['meta']['state_version'] = current_version + 1
    state['meta']['last_modified'] = datetime.now().isoformat()
    
    update_result = delegate_task(
        goal="Update the novel state file after a completed scene",
        context=f"""
        You are the Orchestrator. A scene has just been completed.
        Read the scene's final prose, audit result, and current state,
        then produce a structured update.
        
        CURRENT STATE: Read from novel_state.yaml
        COMPLETED SCENE: Read from scenes/ch{chapter_num:02d}_s{scene_num:02d}_final.md
        AUDIT LOG: {audit_result}
        
        Apply updates using these rules:
        
        1. world.timeline: APPEND new events. Never modify existing entries.
        2. characters[].relationships: UPDATE with explicit old_value/new_value.
        3. characters[].growth.chapters: APPEND new chapter entry.
        4. summaries.chapter_summaries: UPDATE in place (current chapter) or APPEND.
        5. summaries.character_arcs_so_far: UPDATE in place.
        6. chapters[].scenes[].status: SET to "complete".
        7. audit_log: APPEND new entry.
        
        Output the specific changes as a YAML patch. Do NOT rewrite the entire file.
        """,
        toolsets=['terminal', 'file']
    )
    
    # Validate update before applying
    validated = validate_state_update(state, update_result)
    if not validated['ok']:
        raise StateUpdateError(f"State validation failed: {validated['errors']}")
    
    # Apply patch
    state = apply_state_patch(state, update_result)
    return state


def validate_state_update(current_state, update_patch):
    """Validate a state update before applying it."""
    errors = []
    
    # Check for contradictions in timeline
    current_events = {(e['chapter'], e['scene'], e['event']) 
                      for e in current_state['world']['timeline']}
    new_events = update_patch.get('world', {}).get('timeline', [])
    for event in new_events:
        key = (event['chapter'], event['scene'], event['event'])
        if key in current_events:
            errors.append(f"Duplicate timeline event: {key}")
    
    # Check character update old_values match current state
    char_updates = update_patch.get('characters', [])
    for update in char_updates:
        char_id = update.get('id')
        field_path = update.get('field_path', '')
        old_value = update.get('old_value')
        # Navigate to field in current_state
        current_value = get_nested_value(current_state, field_path)
        if current_value != old_value:
            errors.append(
                f"Character {char_id}: old_value mismatch at {field_path}. "
                f"Expected {current_value}, got {old_value}"
            )
    
    # Check required fields
    if 'last_modified' not in update_patch.get('meta', {}):
        errors.append("Missing meta.last_modified")
    
    return {'ok': len(errors) == 0, 'errors': errors}
```

### Why This Approach Works

- `delegate_task` gives each agent a clean context — no accumulated confusion
- File-based state means any agent can read the full picture (within context budget)
- YAML is human-readable and human-editable (you can intervene manually)
- No infrastructure needed — works with just Hermes and a filesystem
- Easy to inspect intermediate outputs (scene briefs, character feedback, audit reports)
- Easy to retry individual steps without redoing the whole pipeline
- Character voice-checks produce authentic dialogue without the overhead of turn-based generation

### What Changed and Why

**Main orchestration pipeline (`generate_novel` + `generate_chapter`)** updated to reflect the simplified loop. The calling sequence now uses `generate_scene_draft` and `collect_character_feedback` instead of the turn-based `generate_scene` function. This reduces per-scene delegate calls from ~18 to ~5. The pipeline makes the correct order explicit and shows the complete scene generation lifecycle. (Lens 1: Architectural Flaws, Lens 3: Over-Engineering)

**`generate_scene` replaced by `generate_scene_draft` + `collect_character_feedback`.** The original `generate_scene` managed per-beat character turns with `get_active_characters` tracking entry/exit. This function and `get_active_characters` have been removed. The new model: Storyteller writes the full draft in one call, then character agents review their lines. The `characters_entering`/`characters_exiting` fields in the scene brief still exist for the Storyteller's reference, but there's no per-beat character loop. (Lens 1: Architectural Flaws)

**`update_state_after_scene` expanded with version checking and validation.** The original function delegated to an agent with a vague "YAML patch" instruction. The new function checks `state_version` before updating, validates the patch for contradictions and old_value mismatches, and raises explicit exceptions on failure. This prevents the silent state corruption that would make multi-chapter generation impossible. (Lens 1: Architectural Flaws, Lens 4: Vague Specifications)

**`validate_state_update` function added.** This was the biggest gap in the original implementation. The plan said "Orchestrator validates the update" but never specified how. The new function checks for duplicate timeline events, character field old_value mismatches, and missing required fields. Without this, bad state updates would cascade silently into all future scenes. (Lens 2: Missing Pieces, Lens 4: Vague Specifications)

**Context overflow guardrail retained unchanged.** The `estimate_context_tokens` and `enforce_context_budget` functions are correct and necessary. (Lens 5: Quality Mechanisms)

**Lore Auditor prompt expanded** to include pacing and theme checks (previously Orchestrator Auditor domain). The prompt structure with explicit `passage` quoting is retained — this is essential for targeted revision. (Lens 4: Vague Specifications)
